The Working Life

Staten Island Helps Define a Native’s First Novel

By Rachel L. Swarns

  • April 12, 2015

On the last day of his legal career, Eddie Joyce packed up his desk and turned in his security badge. He said goodbye to the partners and associates, to his six-figure salary, to his Park Avenue office.

He was 34 years old. His wife was nine months pregnant. And he had just decided to give up his job as a lawyer to try to write short stories and novels. Exhilarated, anxious and uncertain, he was sure of only one thing: That he would write about the borough where he grew up.

It was the place his mind drifted to when he was on the subway in Manhattan or staring at the computer screen in his apartment in Brooklyn Heights: The island with dunes on one end and industrial waterfront on the other. The tight-knit neighborhoods filled with police officers, teachers, firefighters and secretaries. The summertime splashes in backyard swimming pools, the sweat and hustle on gritty basketball courts, the flickering candles at Sunday morning Mass.

A literary work about Staten Island? That’s right. Last month, Mr. Joyce’s debut, “Small Mercies,” published by Viking, arrived in bookstores around the country. The New York Times described it as “an intimate family portrait.” Richard Russo, the Pulitzer Prize-winning author, hailed it as “a terrific first novel.”

Mr. Joyce, who left the legal profession nearly six years ago, knows that some of you might be conjuring up images of Mafia bosses and New Yawk voices, gold chains and shellacked hair. Maybe you envision the televised caricatures in “Mob Wives” and “Jersey Shore” when you think about his borough — if you think about it at all.

But that is not the Staten Island he knows. It is not the community that shaped him as a writer or the borough he wants to introduce to the world.

Mr. Joyce’s accent has faded. His childhood home in Tottenville has been torn down. His parents and siblings have moved to Brooklyn and New Jersey. It has been more than 16 years since he lived in the borough of his birth. But sometimes a place seeps into your psyche, even when you’re not looking, even when you’re long gone.

So Mr. Joyce populated his novel with working-class and middle-class people like his parents, his aunts and uncles, his friends and neighbors. And in his tale of an Irish-Italian family grappling with the aftermath of the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, he brings to life an island that is often slighted and overlooked in the city and its literature.

“I always knew that the place was going to be an extremely important part of the book, that in some ways it would almost define the book,” Mr. Joyce, now 39, explained over coffee at Mikey Bagels in Tottenville.

“When you come from someplace like Staten Island and you go out in the world, you either feel pride or shame or some mixture of the two,” he said. “I don’t recall feeling shame. I’m sure I felt some tiny dollop of it. I’m almost defiantly proud to be from Staten Island.”

I grew up on Staten Island, and to me his portrait of this small town in the big city feels immediately familiar: Young people, some adrift, some burning with purpose, cruise the bars on Forest Avenue and take classes at the College of Staten Island. There are receptions at the Staaten catering hall and dates at Denino’s pizzeria, kitchen tables loaded with steaming plates of pasta and the latest edition of The Staten Island Advance.

There are strong women who hold their families together and children who struggle to find their way. Some flee the island’s sometimes suffocating insularity. Others, like Frank, a character in the novel “whose great ambition was tomorrow night,” never leave.

It is not, thankfully, a romanticized view. Mr. Joyce also describes the racial bigotry that sometimes spills into barroom banter, the divide between the predominantly white south shore and the more ethnically diverse north, and the occasional battles with drink and depression.

And he vividly depicts the sense of alienation that some Staten Islanders share, the feeling of being outsiders in their own city, of being disdained and misunderstood. (Other Staten Island writers have also found inspiration in our forgotten borough, including Paul Zindel, who won the Pulitzer Prize in 1971 for his Broadway play, “The Effect of Gamma Rays on Man-in-the-Moon Marigolds.” )

Mr. Joyce said he never forgot that feeling “of being the underdog,” even after he graduated from Tottenville High School and moved on to Harvard University and Georgetown University Law Center.

He lives comfortably, thanks to his wife’s rewarding legal career, and his budding success has given him hope that he can make something of this writing life.

But he has not lost his appreciation for Staten Islanders like his mother and father, a teacher and a bar owner, “the people who work hard and don’t ask for much and aren’t given much.”

Mr. Russo, who has championed Mr. Joyce’s book, said Staten Island emerges as an “exquisitely rendered” character in the novel, as do the blue-collar men and women who live there.

“He doesn’t look at these lives like a sociologist would,” Mr. Russo said. “He knows them from the inside.”
