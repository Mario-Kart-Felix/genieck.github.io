class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill" style="padding-bottom: 49.7%;"></div><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*mA_7HhiDyWqsB9Oj.jpg" data-width="589" data-height="293" Our world is building a record of the History of the Gods… </em>now a map to our own future.</p><p name="827d" id="827d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Sealed with symbols from the future, I’ve written three books so far. Here, for the sake of explaining the Sodium seal and it’s link to Adam and Eve I will tie together several Earthly manifestations that serve to illuminate the ties that bind Sumerian, Egyptian, and JudeoChristian religions through <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">people</em>.</p><h3 name="125b" id="125b" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Jesus Christ is Tammuz-Adonis</h3><p name="9fc1" id="9fc1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">In Babylonia, the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tammuz_%28Babylonian_calendar%29" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tammuz_(Babylonian_calendar)" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Tammuz (Babylonian calendar)" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">month Tammuz</a> was established in honor of the eponymous god Tammuz, who originated as a Sumerian shepherd-god, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumuzid_the_Shepherd" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumuzid_the_Shepherd" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Dumuzid the Shepherd" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Dumuzid</a> or Dumuzi, the consort of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inanna" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inanna" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Inanna" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Inanna</a> and, in his Akkadian form, the parallel consort of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishtar" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishtar" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Ishtar" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Ishtar</a>. It is the link between Paradise Island, Solomon Kershner and the idiom “No man becomes an island,” which ties Esau and the Kingdom of Edom to this place… where the truth is written in the most indelible place it can be… the Akashic records of the Universe, our home.</p><figure name="c6ea" id="c6ea" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 620px; max-height: 403px;"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill" style="padding-bottom: 65%;"></div><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*JMa_5TyCG5prx9OP." data-width="620" data-height="403" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*JMa_5TyCG5prx9OP."></div><figcaption class="imageCaption">ha.lamc.la</figcaption></figure><figure name="5904" id="5904" class="graf graf--figure graf--iframe graf-after--figure"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill" style="padding-bottom: 56.2%;"></div><div class="iframeContainer"><IFRAME data-width="854" data-height="480" width="700" height="393" src="/media/e6cfa7e6d97088b990acc7d33440fbe8?postId=317cd4967339" data-media-id="e6cfa7e6d97088b990acc7d33440fbe8" allowfullscreen frameborder="0"></IFRAME></div></div></figure><h3 name="ce92" id="ce92" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--figure">Do you understand who I am, do you want to know?</h3><h4 name="ee3b" id="ee3b" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--h3">Conflict of Horus and Set</h4><p name="7e33" id="7e33" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">The next phase of the myth begins when the adult Horus challenges Set for the throne of Egypt. The contest between them is often violent but is also described as a legal judgment before the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ennead" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ennead" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Ennead" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Ennead</a>, an assembled group of Egyptian deities, to decide who should <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_succession" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_succession" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Royal succession" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">inherit</a> the kingship. The judge in this trial may be Geb, who, as the father of Osiris and Set, held the throne before they did, or it may be the creator gods Ra or Atum, the originators of kingship.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-58" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-58" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[58]</a> Other deities also take important roles: Thoth frequently acts as a conciliator in the dispute<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-59" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-59" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[59]</a> or as an assistant to the divine judge, and in “Contendings”, Isis uses her cunning and magical power to aid her son.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-60" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-60" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[60]</a></p><p name="a5e8" id="a5e8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The rivalry of Horus and Set is portrayed in two contrasting ways. Both perspectives appear as early as the <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Pyramid Texts</em>, the earliest source of the myth. In some spells from these texts, Horus is the son of Osiris and nephew of Set, and the murder of Osiris is the major impetus for the conflict. The other tradition depicts Horus and Set as brothers.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-61" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-61" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[61]</a>This incongruity persists in many of the subsequent sources, where the two gods may be called brothers or uncle and nephew at different points in the same text.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-Assmann_134-62" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-Assmann_134-62" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[62]</a></p><figure name="dfca" id="dfca" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 220px; max-height: 293px;"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill" style="padding-bottom: 133.20000000000002%;"></div><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*--5CIqsmvYG-4G3Q.JPG" data-width="220" data-height="293" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*--5CIqsmvYG-4G3Q.JPG"></div></figure><p name="b22d" id="b22d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Horus spears Set, who appears in the form of a hippopotamus, as Isis looks on</p><p name="e166" id="e166" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The divine struggle involves many episodes. “Contendings” describes the two gods appealing to various other deities to arbitrate the dispute and competing in different types of contests, such as racing in boats or fighting each other in the form of hippopotami, to determine a victor. In this account, Horus repeatedly defeats Set and is supported by most of the other deities.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-63" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-63" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[63]</a> Yet the dispute drags on for eighty years, largely because the judge, the creator god, favors Set.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-64" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-64" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[64]</a> In late ritual texts, the conflict is characterized as a great battle involving the two deities’ assembled followers.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-65" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-65" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[65]</a> The strife in the divine realm extends beyond the two combatants. At one point Isis attempts to harpoon Set as he is locked in combat with her son, but she strikes Horus instead, who then cuts off her head in a fit of rage.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-66" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-66" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[66]</a> Thoth replaces Isis’s head with that of a cow; the story gives a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_myth" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_myth" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Origin myth" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">mythical origin</a> for the cow-horn headdress that Isis commonly wears.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-67" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-67" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[67]</a></p><p name="4b49" id="4b49" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In a key episode in the conflict, Set sexually abuses Horus. Set’s violation is partly meant to degrade his rival, but it also involves homosexual desire, in keeping with one of Set’s major characteristics, his forceful and indiscriminate sexuality.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-68" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-68" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[68]</a> In the earliest account of this episode, in a fragmentary Middle Kingdom papyrus, the sexual encounter begins when Set asks to have sex with Horus, who agrees on the condition that Set will give Horus some of his strength.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-69" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-69" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[69]</a> The encounter puts Horus in danger, because in Egyptian tradition semen is a potent and dangerous substance, akin to poison. According to some texts, Set’s semen enters Horus’s body and makes him ill, but in “Contendings”, Horus thwarts Set by catching Set’s semen in his hands. Isis retaliates by putting Horus’s semen on lettuce-leaves that Set eats. Set’s defeat becomes apparent when this semen appears on his forehead as a golden disk. He has been impregnated with his rival’s seed and as a result “gives birth” to the disk. In “Contendings”, Thoth takes the disk and places it on his own head; in earlier accounts, it is Thoth who is produced by this anomalous birth.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-70" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-70" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[70]</a></p><p name="67c4" id="67c4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Another important episode concerns mutilations that the combatants inflict upon each other: Horus injures or steals Set’s testicles and Set damages or tears out one, or occasionally both, of Horus’s eyes. Sometimes the eye is torn into pieces.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-Pinch_82-71" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-Pinch_82-71" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[71]</a> Set’s mutilation signifies a loss of virility and strength.<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-72" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris_myth#cite_note-72" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">[72]</a> The removal of Horus’s eye is even more important, for this stolen <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" title="Eye of Horus" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Eye of Horus</a> represents a wide variety of concepts in Egyptian religion. One of Horus’s major roles is as a sky deity, and for this reason his right eye was said to be the sun and his left eye the moon.</p><h3 name="7e5b" id="7e5b" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Like
